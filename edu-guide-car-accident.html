What to Do After a Car Accident in Georgia

A car accident is one of the most disorienting events of a person's life. In the chaos of the moment, it is easy to make decisions that permanently damage your legal claim. Here is what Georgia personal injury attorneys recommend you do immediately following a collision to protect your rights, establish the at-fault driver's fault, and maximize the average car accident settlement in Georgia you can recover.

Step 1: Move to Safety and Check for Injuries

If your vehicle is operable and it is safe to do so, move it to the shoulder or a nearby parking area. Activate your hazard lights. Check yourself and all passengers for injuries before exiting the vehicle. Do not assume you are uninjured — adrenaline masks pain.

Step 2: Call 911

Georgia law (O.C.G.A. Â§ 40-6-273) requires reporting accidents that result in injury, death, or significant property damage. More importantly, an official police report is one of the most valuable pieces of evidence in a personal injury claim. Do not agree to "settle it between yourselves" without involving law enforcement — the other driver's story will change without a report to contradict them.

Step 3: Document Everything at the Scene

Use your phone to photograph the vehicles from all angles, road conditions, traffic signs, skid marks, and any visible injuries. Capture the other driver's license plate, insurance card, and driver's license. Note the badge number of responding officers. Collect names and contact information from all witnesses before anyone leaves the scene.

Step 4: Seek Medical Attention Immediately

Whiplash, traumatic brain injury, internal bleeding, and soft-tissue injuries may not produce obvious symptoms for 24 to 72 hours after impact. Insurance adjusters routinely argue that any gap between the accident and your first medical visit proves you were not seriously injured. See a doctor the same day — preferably at an emergency room or urgent care facility — and describe every symptom, no matter how minor it seems.

Step 5: Notify Your Insurance Company

Report the accident to your own insurer promptly. Most policies require timely notice as a condition of coverage. Stick to basic facts: date, time, location, vehicles involved. You are not obligated to provide a detailed recorded statement to your own insurer without guidance from an attorney.

Step 6: Decline a Recorded Statement to the Other Driver's Insurer

The other driver's insurance adjuster may call within hours of the accident and request a recorded statement. You are under no legal obligation to provide one. Everything you say will be analyzed for inconsistencies, minimizations of injury, or admissions of fault — all of which reduce your settlement. Politely decline and contact a Georgia personal injury attorney first.

Step 7: Contact a Georgia Personal Injury Attorney

Evidence degrades fast. Security footage is overwritten within days. Witnesses forget details. Skid marks and debris are cleared. The sooner you retain an attorney, the better your ability to preserve the evidence that wins your case. This is especially true if you were struck by a distracted driver in Georgia, where fast device subpoenaing is critical.
